Abstract

Multimodal learning enables models to process and reason
over diverse information sources, unlocking human-like perceptual and cognitive capabilities. As such models gain adoption, e!ciently serving them on GPUs has become increasingly important. However, the modular architecture of multimodal models poses signi"cant challenges to existing unimodal serving systems, which treat models as monolithic and
overlook inter-module heterogeneity. This results in severe
GPU underutilization. To address this, we propose E!"!!, a
multimodal serving system based on a new scheduling paradigm we call module multiplexing. Unlike prior approaches
that execute all modules sequentially with uniform batch
sizes, E!"!! schedules modality-speci"c modules concurrently on the same GPU with independently tuned batching
and resource allocation. This design enables "ne-grained
GPU sharing, boosting intra-GPU parallelism and improving request-level throughput. We implement a prototype of
E!"!! and evaluate it on several representative multimodal
models (e.g., CLIP, BLIP, LLaVA, InternVL). Our results show
that E!"!! signi"cantly outperforms state-of-the-art serving
systems in both throughput and GPU utilization.

Introduction

Multimodal learning is widely regarded as a transformative
step toward arti"cial general intelligence, aiming to develop
models capable of ingesting, interpreting, and reasoning over
diverse information sources. Driven by advances in transformer architectures, a range of powerful pre-trained multimodal models have emerged, including CLIP [44], BLIP [32],
BLIP-2 [31], and LLaVA [35], which have fueled a broad
spectrum of downstream applications [3, 22, 33, 54, 58].
As these models become increasingly prevalent, the need
for e!cient multimodal model serving—achieving low latency and high resource utilization—has become critical.
While prior work has made signi"cant progress in developing
model serving systems, such as TF-Serving [40], Clipper [12],
TurboTransformers [18], and Triton [39], these systems are
primarily designed for unimodal models and fall short when
serving complex multimodal architectures.
These systems typically treat a model as a monolithic
computation graph, executing each layer sequentially with
a uniform batch size. To improve GPU utilization, they scale
up the batch size until the inference latency violates the
Service-Level Objective (SLO). However, such strategies become ine!cient when applied to multimodal models. For
example, our preliminary experiments (Figure 4 in § 3) show
that GPU utilization drops below 10% for over 50% of the
time when serving BLIP on a Visual Question Answering
(VQA) task using NVIDIA Triton.
This ine!ciency stems from a fundamental mismatch:
existing unimodal serving systems fail to account for the
intra-model heterogeneity introduced by modality-speci"c

The overall architecture, depicted in Figure 2a, consists of
three modules. The vision module uses ViT [16] as the image encoder to extract visual features. The two text modules
are based on BERT [14]: one is a text encoder that extracts
text features, and the other is a text decoder that generates
text output. Each module includes a set of self-attention and
feedforward networks to capture contextual relationships
within its respective modality. Some modules incorporate
self-attention variants (e.g., early summation, hierarchical attention, cross-attention) to enable cross-modal interactions.
In BLIP, the text encoder and text decoder employ crossattention to integrate visual and textual information. As
shown in Figure 2b, another task, Natural Language Visual
Reasoning (NLVR), determines if a sentence corresponds to
a description of a pair of images [48].
Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). The remarkable capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) have
inspired e#orts to integrate them with existing multimodal
models [53]. An MLLM can be conceptualized as a combination of several modules: multiple encoders for di#erent input
modalities, a pre-trained LLM, and multiple decoders for outputting various modalities. As shown in Figure 3, BLIP-2 [31]
uses ViT [16] alongside a lightweight transformer called QFormer as its visual encoder, with Flan-T5 [11] or OPT [56] as
its LLM. LLaVA replaces the Q-Former structure with a single
linear layer [35]. NExT-GPT [51] utilizes ImageBind [21], a
versatile six-modality encoder, and various di#usion models
for di#erent modal generations, including Stable Di#usion
(SD) for image synthesis, Zeroscope for video synthesis, and
AudioLDM for audio synthesis.
MTMs vs MLLMs. The emergence of MLLMs does not
mean that traditional MTMs should be discarded. MTMs
require fewer resources and achieve relatively good performance across a wide range of multimodal tasks. As such, they
are well suited for edge scenarios, and this paper considers
both MTMs and MLLMs. As many MTMs and MLLMs follow
similar architectures such as BLIP and BLIP-2 respectively,
our paper focuses mainly on their serving.

Batching. It combines multiple model inference requests
into a single batch to improve GPU utilisation. This takes advantage of the parallel processing capabilities of the GPU to
process multiple inputs simultaneously and e!ciently reduce
idle time. E#ective batching requires careful consideration of
batch size, latency constraints and workload characteristics.
The challenge is to balance latency and throughput, as overly
large batches can result in unacceptable delays for end users.
GPU Multiplexing. It allows multiple workloads to share
GPU. This involves leveraging the GPU’s Streaming Multiprocessors (SMs), the units executing parallel tasks. Temporal
sharing schedules di#erent tasks simultaneously, maintaining consistent GPU activity without resource contention.
Spatial sharing enables multiple tasks to run concurrently
by distributing workloads across SMs. Multi-Process Service
(MPS) [37] and multi-instance GPU (MIG) [36] enables simultaneous execution of multiple models. This paper is based on
MPS because MIG provides only rigid partitioning, leading
to GPU overprovisioning and underutilization, and is only
available in the latest generation of GPUs.

In unimodal language-only models, the input is text, which

converting text into token sequences, embedding them numerically, and assembling them into tensors. Since tokenization introduces negligible latency, unimodal serving typically

Module Caching Control

5.1

Module Computation Caching

A fundamental consequence of module multiplexing is that
the execution of modules no longer follows a strict sequential
order. Unlike traditional serving systems—where intermediate outputs are immediately forwarded to the next module—module multiplexing introduces temporal gaps between
upstream and downstream modules. As a result, the outputs
of earlier modules must be temporarily cached until their
consumers are scheduled. This introduces non-trivial memory and I/O overhead that must be carefully managed.
To support this, E!"!! equips each GPU with a controller
that manages intermediate results at module granularity. The
controller identi"es reusable outputs, caches them across requests, and orchestrates their e!cient retrieval when needed.
In addition to enabling asynchronous execution, this design
also supports reuse of shared computations—such as avoiding redundant visual encoding for repeated inputs—thus
improving both e!ciency and throughput.

System Overview

The overall architecture of E!"!!, illustrated in Figure 10, is
designed to fully realize the bene"ts of module multiplexing.
E!"!! operates on one or more GPUs to serve multimodal
models and handle task requests from multiple users, including both context-sensitive and context-free queries involving
visual inputs. E!"!! consists of two main components: a controller (§ 5) and a scheduler (§ 6). On each GPU, multiple
processes are launched, each dedicated to a speci"c module
of the multimodal model and con"gured with individualized
batch sizes and SM allocations. GPU multiplexing enables
these module processes to execute concurrently, enabling
"ne-grained scheduling and improved resource utilization.
To address the temporal decoupling introduced by module
multiplexing—where downstream modules may not immediately follow upstream ones—a controller is deployed on
each GPU. It maintains both an active cache for ongoing
requests and a historical cache for completed ones. The controller manages the reuse of cached module outputs while
balancing the compute demands of new requests with the
I/O overhead associated with cache access and eviction.

that are reusable across multi-turn interactions as the modal
cache. The design of the modal cache depends on the model’s
input type, internal structure, and inter-module dependencies. While we take vision-language models as an example in
this section, the approach generalizes to other modalities. In

Adapting to Dynamic Workloads

Our o%ine scheduling algorithm is designed to compute optimal strategies for di#erent combinations of GPU, model, and
SLO requirements. For each con"guration, it determines the
maximum sustainable request throughput (i.e., max capacity)
that satis"es SLO. If the incoming request rate exceeds this
max capacity, the system responds by allocating additional
GPUs and switching to the corresponding precomputed strategy. It eliminates the need to re-run the scheduler at runtime
and ensures e!cient adaptation to dynamic workloads.
For e!cient switching, E!"!! employs a standard active–standby switching mechanism, similar to other MPS
systems [14]. When the request pattern drifts beyond a preset
threshold, the system launches a standby MPS instance with
the closest pre-pro"le strategy. Both instances run concurrently during transition, and tra!c is atomically redirected
to the new one. Since model weights are already resident
in memory, switching only updates scheduler metadata and
completes within a few milliseconds. The threshold is computed using a sliding window over recent distribution. If
no close match is found, E!"!! falls back to a conservative
default strategy to ensure robustness.

to these works, we also observe the heterogeneity of the
modules in a multimodal model. However, most of them aim
to mitigate the negative in$uence of module heterogeneity
on the parallelism paradigms of distributed training for multimodal models. Meta [30] evaluates the performance of its
multimodal models and analyzes the e#ectiveness of some
system-level and workload-speci"c optimizations applied
to them. ModServe [43], a concurrent work, also targets ef"cient multimodal serving but adopts a di#erent strategy.
ModServe places di#erent modules on separate GPUs, using a
method conceptually similar to pre"ll-decode disaggregation.
In contrast, our method focuses on intra-GPU scheduling
and SM-level optimization. Both approaches are orthogonal,
addressing complementary aspects of the system design, and
could be integrated in future work.

Batching. Many serving systems utilize batching techniques
to enhance inference e!ciency, including TF-serving [40],
Clipper [12], TurboTransformers [18], and Triton [39]. They
typically employ traditional batching strategies, which rely
on batch time windows and a maximum allowed batch size,
treating the model as an indivisible whole. A closely related
approach is DVABatch [13], which optimizes batching by
accounting for input, operator, and load diversity within a
model. DVABatch enables operators within a model to execute at di#erent batch sizes based on their computational
complexity. However, while e#ective for unimodal models,
DVABatch does not adapt its batching strategies or GPU
multiplexing to the intricate internal structures and interdependencies characteristic of modules in a multimodal model.
GPU Multiplexing. To improve GPU utilization, some approaches load multiple models into GPU memory to share a
single GPU [7, 15, 46, 47, 55]. For example, Choi et al. propose
a spatio-temporal multiplexing system for multi-model ML
inference servers [10], while Duan et al. introduce MuxServe,
a spatio-temporal multiplexing system speci"cally designed
for e!cient serving of multiple LLMs [17]. However, these
spatio-temporal GPU sharing techniques are limited to resource competition among independent models and fail to
adapt to the synergistic interactions between di#erent modules within a multimodal model, as discussed in § 6.

Conclusion

We present E!"!!, an e!cient multimodal model serving system that adopts a module multiplexing approach. E!"!! performs "ne-grained scheduling across modality-speci"c modules by independently managing their computation and resource allocation. This design signi"cantly improves GPU utilization and system throughput. Experimental results show
that E!"!! improves throughput by 157%, reduces latency
by 90%, and achieves up to 90% GPU utilization under both

of multimodal models, such as vision-language-action (VLA)
